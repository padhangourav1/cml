Dr. Vinay Bulusu

Assistant Professor, Biological Sciences, IISER Berhampur

I am a biochemist interested in understanding the metabolic organization and regulation of cellular processes in cancer and embryonic development. Our group uses a combination of functional genomics with advanced mass spectrometry (MS-Metabolomics), imaging, protein-based metabolite biosensors, and computational modelling to understand the metabolic cross-talk with signaling and gene transcription networks.

Our Research

While the role of thermodynamic and kinetic principles in organizing and driving metabolic reactions are understood, the specific interplay of metabolites and enzymes in modulating signaling and gene transcription is now coming to the fore. During embryonic development and in metabolic diseases such as cancer, cellular fates like cell proliferation and differentiation are associated with specific metabolic states. We are focused on answering several key questions in this domain:

1. How does sub-cellular metabolic compartmentalization help in cancer progression?

In solid tumors, cancer cells rewire their metabolism to survive nutrient limitation. We've shown that acetate is scavenged by hypoxic cancer cells. While acetate was thought to feed a common nucleo-cytosolic acetyl-CoA pool, our work revealed that under hypoxic conditions, these pools are compartmentalized by a kinetic barrier. This barrier channels exogenous acetate directly into lipid synthesis, bypassing histone acetylation. We are now pursuing further studies to understand such flux barriers and their role in cancer progression.

2. How do dynamic changes in metabolites effect gene expression networks?

Metabolites such as acetyl-CoA and S-adenosyl methionine are crucial cofactors for enzymes that modify DNA and chromatin. The link between fluctuations in these metabolites and resulting changes in gene expression is a key area of investigation. We are currently developing novel, genetically encoded fluorescence-based sensors for these metabolites. Using protein engineering and modelling, we aim to visualize and understand their sub-cellular dynamics in real-time.

3. What is the role of metabolic gradients in cancer and embryonic development?

Altered metabolism in cancer cells creates metabolic gradients within tumors. We have previously demonstrated the existence of a glycolytic gradient in the mouse embryonic presomitic mesoderm, which is essential for axis elongation and patterning. Such gradients may provide robustness against nutrient fluctuations. We are currently dissecting the mechanisms of gradient formation and function using both in vitro cell culture models and future in vivo mouse models.
